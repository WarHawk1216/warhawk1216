Discourses and Selected Writings —Epictetus

A collection of writings and conversations between Epictetus and his academy, which were recorded by his student Arrian. Epictetus was an early stoic also responsible for the writing of the Enchiridion.


Discourses is an anthology of practical philosophical teachings which are meant to be employed in one's life, as taught by Epictetus. Some of my favourite Stoic teachings are present in this this book. One of which is an anecdote of Diogenes:


How we should struggle with circumstance "[6] Diogenes went scouting before you did and came back with a very different report. Death, he said, was note evil becuase it was not dishonourable. Reputation was the empty noise of fools. [7] And he said other things that helped remove the elemenet of fear from pain and poverty. In his manner of life he preferred the minimum of clothing to a purple gown, and the bare ground to a bed, however soft. [8] And as proof of such claims, he produced his assurance, his serenity, his freedom - as well as his tough, radiant physique."

The Denial of Death —Ernest Becker

This is one of my favourite, if not favourite, books ever written. Despite many psychologists, philosophers, academics, and the like crowd claiming that Becker is wrong, the book still stands as a testament to Becker's amazing capability to synthesize subjects otherwise loosely related into a very compelling and cohesive piece of work which aims to 'solve,' or discusses the potential of solving, the problem of living and dying. I began reading the book because I was familiar with the existentialist philosopher SÃ¸ren Kierkegaard (one of the most brilliant philosophers I have ever come across) and through some research learned that Becker's book was somewhat of an expansion on Kierkegaard's ideas. What it was in actuality was far more than such, it was a composure consisting of numerous texts and ideas that originate from the (at times) opposing fields of philosophy and psychology; somewhat of an irony regarding this is that Carl Jung, who's mentioned occasionaly, had a distaste for philosophy and Kierkegaard in particular. I will definetely be reading this book again in a year or two because one reading is not enough to understand Becker's thought in its entirety.
